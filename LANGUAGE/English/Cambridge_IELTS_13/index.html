    IELTS Speaking Preparation: Part 1 - Television Programmes¶

    In the first part of the IELTS Speaking test, the examiner will introduce themselves and confirm your identity. They will then ask you questions related to your studies, work, accommodation, or other familiar topics. Below are some example questions and sample answers on the topic of Television Programmes.

    1. Where do you usually watch TV programmes/shows? [Why?/Why not?]¶

    Sample Answer:

    Well, I think nowadays I hardly ever watch live TV on the actual TV set. Instead, I have subscribed to a few online TV streaming sites, so I can easily watch the shows I like wherever I want, on my phone or laptop, completely commercial-free, which is another important advantage.

    Advanced Vocabulary and Phrases:

    • hardly ever /ˈhɑːrdli ˈɛvər/: 几乎没有
    • online TV streaming sites /ˈɒnˌlaɪn tiː viː ˈstriːmɪŋ saɪts/: 网络电视
    • subscribe to /səbˈskraɪb tuː/: 订阅
    • commercial-free /kəˈmɜːrʃəl friː/: 无广告
    • phone /fəʊn/: 手机
    • laptop /ˈlæpˌtɒp/: 笔记本电脑

    2. What's your favorite TV programme/show? [Why?]¶

    Sample Answer:

    Much as I hate to admit it, I am a reality TV junkie! I also watch stuff like comedies and crime TV programmes. But you could burn the house down around me while my favorite reality show is on! I know most of the shows are of little value, but I just can't help it!

    Advanced Vocabulary and Phrases:

    • junkie /ˈdʒʌŋki/: [俚语] 比喻对某事痴迷的人
    • reality TV /riˈælɪti tiː viː/: 真人秀
    • crime TV programme /kraɪm tiː viː ˈprəʊɡræm/: 犯罪剧
    • comedies /ˈkɒmədiz/: 喜剧
    • burn the house down around me /bɜːrn ðə haʊs daʊn əˈraʊnd miː/: [比喻] 完全无视周遭
    • can't help it /kænt hɛlp ɪt/: 无法自拔

    3. Are there any programmes/shows you don't like watching? [Why?/Why not?]¶

    Sample Answer:

    Well, it would have to be soaps. They are just so predictable and boring. I prefer shows like the British TV series Black Mirror. There's always a twist at the end which will leave you wanting more. And I like those dystopian stories; they really make you think.

    Advanced Vocabulary and Phrases:

    • soaps /soʊps/: 肥皂剧
    • predictable /prɪˈdɪktəbl/: 可预测的
    • boring /ˈbɔːrɪŋ/: 无聊的
    • Black Mirror /blæk ˈmɪrər/: 英国电视剧《黑镜》
    • twist /twɪst/: 情节反转
    • wanting more /ˈwɒntɪŋ mɔːr/: 想要更多
    • dystopian stories /dɪsˈtəʊpiən ˈstɔːriz/: 反乌托邦的故事
    • make you think /meɪk juː θɪŋk/: 引人思考

    4. Do you think you will watch more TV or fewer TV programmes/shows in the future? [Why?/Why not?]¶

    Sample Answer:

    I'd like to say fewer! I mean, I know I'd be better off spending time on something more productive. In fact, whenever I binge-watch some hit shows, I feel guilty. But I don't think I could simply quit it because if I did, I'd miss out on so many things that my friends would be talking about.

    Advanced Vocabulary and Phrases:

    • fewer /ˈfjuːər/: 更少
    • better off /ˈbɛtər ɒf/: 更好
    • productive /prəˈdʌktɪv/: 产生价值的
    • binge-watch /bɪnʤ ˈwɒʧ/: 一次看很多集
    • miss out on /mɪs aʊt ɒn/: 错过

    Note: Practicing these responses and understanding the advanced vocabulary will help enhance your fluency and coherence during the IELTS Speaking test.

    IELTS Speaking Preparation: Part 2 - Describe Someone You Know Who Has Started a Business¶

    Describe someone you know who has started a business.

    You should say:

    • who this person is
    • what work this person does
    • why this person decided to start a business
    • and explain whether you would like to do the same kind of work as this person.

    Sample Answer:

    In recent years, there has been a surge in entrepreneurship in China, and it seems that everybody is contemplating starting a business of some kind. However, the person I'd like to talk about is truly a pioneer—his name is Mr. Luo.

    First, I'll explain what he does. Originally, Mr. Luo was an anchor at Chinese Central Television, a job many people would kill for. However, he noticed an opportunity when more and more Chinese people began using WeChat, an instant messaging and social networking app. Seizing this opportunity, he quit his stable job and embarked on his entrepreneurial journey. He started by opening an account to share book reviews and mini lectures on WeChat. Gradually, his account amassed such a huge following that he expanded his venture by launching a website. On this platform, he collaborates with well-known writers and professors to offer online courses for a fee.

    Contrary to the popular belief that people won't pay for anything they read online, Mr. Luo's business has gone from strength to strength. Not only have people been willing to “pay for knowledge,” but they have also remained very loyal to his website, eagerly consuming everything that was on offer. Of course, Mr. Luo's success was no accident. He didn't gain attention right away; instead, he made a name for himself by delivering a high-quality mini lecture every single morning for two years straight. Additionally, his keen insight into the needs of China's rising middle class—who are hungry for information and knowledge that they believe would help them increase their wealth or improve the quality of their lives—played a significant role in his success.

    I wouldn't say that he is my role model because I think I lack the business acumen and charisma he possesses. However, I do admire his persistence and courage to challenge the status quo. While I don't believe I could do the same kind of work as he does, I would love to start a course on his website one day if I had the chance. Although I think there's little likelihood of this happening because I don't think I've got what it takes to be a lecturer!

    IELTS Speaking Preparation: Part 3 - Choosing Work¶

    1. What kinds of jobs do young people not want to do in your country?¶

    Sample Answer:

    Well, I guess it really depends on one's circumstances. But generally, I think the younger generation has received better education than their parents’ generation, so they tend to have higher opinions of themselves and refuse to do jobs that they think are beneath them, such as blue-collar jobs or service jobs. On the other hand, most young people today would prefer to see the world and work in a big city rather than be stuck in a small town where they were born, even though their parents may have had a good life planed out for them—like working as a civil servant in the local government, marrying early, and buying a house near the best school in town. I guess it's fortunate that not every child chooses to follow in their parents' footsteps. After all, this is how society progresses, isn't it?

    Advanced Vocabulary and Phrases:

    • depends on one's circumstances /dɪˈpɛndz ɒn wʌnz ˌsɜːrkəmˈstænsɪz/: 取决于个人境遇
    • younger generation /ˈjʌŋər ˌdʒɛnəˈreɪʃən/: 年轻一代
    • higher opinions of themselves /ˈhaɪər əˈpɪnjənz əv ðɛmˈsɛlvz/: 自视甚高
    • beneath them /bɪˈniːθ ðɛm/: 配不上他们的
    • blue-collar jobs /bluː ˈkɒlər dʒɒbz/: 蓝领工作
    • service jobs /ˈsɜːrvɪs dʒɒbz/: 服务性工作
    • see the world /siː ðə wɜːrld/: 去看世界
    • stuck /stʌk/: 被困
    • civil servant /ˈsɪvəl ˈsɜːrvənt/: 公务员
    • life planned out /laɪf plænd aʊt/: 设计好的人生
    • follow in their parents' footsteps /ˈfɒləʊ ɪn ðɛər ˈpɛərənts ˈfʊtˌstrɛps/: 追随父母的足迹
    • progresses /prəˈɡrɛsiz/: 进步

    2. Who is best at advising young people about choosing a job: teachers or parents?¶

    Sample Answer:

    Well, it's a tough question because I think ultimately it's up to young people themselves to decide what they want to do with their lives. However, I guess both teachers and parents have some useful perspectives to offer. A teacher may know more about the student's academic performance and thus could make a reasonable prediction about their potential in a specific field. On the other hand, a parent may be a better judge of their children's character, and they could also be good role models if they have had fulfilling careers themselves. However, both teachers and parents could be biased, so I believe it's best to listen to different opinions to make a well-informed decision yourself.

    Advanced Vocabulary and Phrases:

    • ultimately /ˌʌltɪˈmeɪtli/: 最终
    • useful perspectives /ˈjuːsfʊl pərsˈpɛktɪvz/: 有用的视角
    • reasonable prediction /ˈriːzənəbəl prɪˈdɪkʃən/: 合理的预测
    • judge of character /dʒʌdʒ əv ˈkærɪktər/: 善于识人的人
    • role models /roʊl ˈmɒdlz/: 榜样
    • fulfilling careers /fʊlˈfɪlɪŋ kəˈrɪərz/: 满足感的职业
    • biased /ˈbaɪəst/: 偏颇的，片面的
    • listen to different opinions /ˈlɪsən tuː ˈdɪfərənt əˈpɪnjənz/: 听取不同意见
    • well-informed decision /ˌwɛl ɪnˈfɔːrmd dɪˈsɪʒən/: 明智的选择

    3. Is money always the most important thing when choosing a job? Why or why not?¶

    Sample Answer:

    Definitely not! I mean, even if you believe in materialism and think more money will bring more happiness, it would still be very short-sighted to prioritize money over other factors like your passion, career path, work-life balance, etc. Because in the long run, it is those factors that will ensure you're enjoying your life. In fact, I'd argue that money should probably come last when choosing a job, especially for a new graduate. I think it's very important to think long term and remember that everybody has to pay their dues if they want to be successful. There's a saying that goes, “do what you love and the money will follow." I think it makes sense because if you really find your passion, you'll be highly motivated to work hard and think creatively, and eventually be more successful at what you do.

    Advanced Vocabulary and Phrases:

    • materialism /məˈtɪəriəlɪzəm/: 物质主义
    • short-sighted /ˈʃɔːtˌsaɪtɪd/: 短视的
    • prioritize /praɪˈɒrɪtaɪz/: 把……置于优先
    • passion /ˈpæʃən/: 激情
    • career path /kəˈrɪər pæθ/: 职业发展

    Question: In some cultures, children are often told that they can achieve anything if they try hard enough. What are the advantages and disadvantages of giving children this message? Give reasons for your answer and include any relevant examples from your own knowledge or experience.

    Essay:

    Encouraging children with the message that they can achieve anything through sufficient effort is a prevalent practice in numerous cultures. While this affirmation can inspire and motivate, it also presents potential drawbacks that warrant consideration.

    One notable advantage of this message is that it fosters a strong work ethic and resilience in children. Believing that hard work can overcome obstacles encourages persistence and dedication, which are essential traits for personal and professional success. For instance, a child who is assured that academic excellence is attainable through diligent study is more likely to develop effective study habits and a commitment to learning. Additionally, this positive reinforcement can enhance self-esteem and confidence, enabling children to embrace challenges and pursue their goals with determination.

    However, there are also disadvantages to this approach. Telling children they can achieve anything may set unrealistic expectations, leading to disappointment and frustration when they encounter failures. For example, a child who is consistently told they can become a professional athlete might experience significant stress and anxiety if they lack the necessary talent or suffer injuries. Furthermore, this message can overlook the importance of external factors such as socio-economic status, access to resources, and inherent abilities, which are crucial in achieving success. Consequently, children might develop a skewed perception of reality, believing that effort alone is sufficient to overcome all barriers.

    In my opinion, while encouraging children to strive for their goals is beneficial, it is equally important to educate them about the complexities and challenges they may face. Balancing motivation with realistic expectations can help children develop both ambition and resilience.
